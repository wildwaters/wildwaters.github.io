Rivers and the Origins of States in Mesopotamia

  • “sedentism” begins 10,000 YA in Mesopotamia, agriculture fairly widespread by 5,500 BCE
  • “states” arise only ~3,300 BCE
  • why the gap?
  • “sedentism” – setting down, building permanent homes
  • “state” – organized polities with rigid social hierarchies, divisions of labour, surplus accumulation
  • (a) if agriculture is so great, why did it take so long to arise?
  • (b) if states are so great, why did it take so long for them to arise

Farming is Hard Work

  • traditional explanation: farming is a stable calorie source in an unstable world
  • looks like a bad deal in Mesopotamia, though!
  • in fact, 5,000 YA Mesopotamia wasn’t an arid desert that needed to be “made bloom”
  • actually it was an incredibly rich alluvial wetlands with many intersecting ecological zones & tremendous wealth of possible nutrition sources:
    • migrating fish (salmon, eels)
    • migrating herds (bison, gazelles)
    • endogenous fish, turtles, edible plants, mollusks, crustaceans,
    • people living here had lots and lots of options
    • this is what leads them to settle down – they are already wealthy
    • so they can experiment with flood-retreat agriculture (not irrigation)
    • only becasue farming doesn’t suck quite so much, do people decide to give it a go.

St. Lawrence

The Champlain Sea

  • 13,000 YA to 10,000 YA
  • depression of region due to glacier weight
  • “isostatic rebound” – over thousands of years, rise of land
  • marine fossils in the region (including cetaceans)
  • also “Lake Iroquis” which enlarged lake ontario all the way to the 1000 islands (where ice blocked exit of water)
    • border between davenport rd & st. clair

The St. Lawrence System

note the three regions:

  • Great lakes
  • main stem
  • estuarine Gulf of St. L

history:

  • pre-contact: heavily settled by many different peoples.
  • eventually at least 3 very lage groups: Cree, Anishinaabe & Haudenosaunee (often called “Iroquois” or “Five” (and then “six”, after 1722) Nations.
    • post American Revolution, British Govt cedes Haudenosaunee territory south of St. L to the AMericans, and many resettle North of the jriver in Upper & Lower Canada
    • Abundance of water & rich hunting teritory led to many flourishing societies with great internal complexities.
  • Note on terminology: First Nation, Native American, “Indian” (note al l3 appear here). vestiges, remnants, impacts

Geology

  • fairly diverse, formed by glaciation over the last ~40K years
  • mostly flat in the West, but some hills and mountains in the East
  • lots of limestone, dolomite (sedimentary rocks) but Canadian Shield in the north.

human geography:

  • about 60M people in the region
  • most very close to the water
  • highly urbanized/industrialized

  • take a moment to think about what the St. Lawrence might have done to human social development in the region

What’s special about the St. Lawrence?

  • 2nd largest flow in NA: 16800 m3/s (7400 m3/s from Lake O)
  • b/c of lakes, water is clearer
  • supports millions of people
  • very highly regulated (like many other rivers)
  • lots of “anadromous” and at least one important “catadromous” species

Upper “fluvial” region

  • no tidal effets
  • large impacts of lakes
  • largest number of hydro-electric projects
  • most significant dredging

How has settler socety affected the basin?

  • removal of beavers
  • deforestation
  • industrial point-source pollution
  • agricultural run-off
  • urbanization, asphault, and dispersed pollutant sources
  • Dams!
  • “strongly fragmented” system w/ sometimes only 1/4 of the flow unimpeded by dams

American Eel

  • “catadromous”
  • 1980: 25,000/day
  • 2000: 250/day
  • 50% of early c20 biomass in Lake O. Active fishery

Zebra Mussel

  • native to Russia, Ukraine
  • entered St. Lawrence in 1980’s in ballast
  • filtration of water → light penetration → algal blooms

Ottawa River

  • origins in Canadian Shield (granitic, hard rock)
  • discharge of 1950m3/s
  • many threatened fish species
  • histories of E. Coli and PCB
  • PCBs:
    • cancer
    • developmental neruological effects
    • hormone disruption (perhaps related)
    • “bio-accumulation”
